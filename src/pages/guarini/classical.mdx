You have met two knight-swap puzzles so far, and both yielded to
patience: first the 3×3 diagonal swap, then the ten-square irregular
board. So here, at last, is the puzzle that started it all — the one
Italian mathematician Paolo Guarini di Forlì set down in 1512.

Four knights on a 3×3 board. The two red knights sit on the top two
corners; the two blue knights on the bottom two corners. Your task is
the one Guarini himself posed: rearrange the knights, one legal
knight-jump at a time, until the reds occupy the squares where the
blues started, and the blues occupy the squares where the reds did.
Two knights may never share a square.

Guarini did not say whether it can be done.

Play with it for as long as you like before reading on. Try short
sequences, try long ones, try reasoning about where any given knight
could possibly go. Count moves, count dead ends, notice patterns. If
you find a solution, excellent — you have out-done half a millennium
of puzzlers. If you begin to suspect there is no solution at all,
that is interesting in its own right: how would you *prove* such a
thing, without testing every sequence of moves?

If you would like a hand, the **Mind over matter** button reveals a
visual aid that has helped mathematicians crack many puzzles like this
one. Whether or not it reveals the answer is for you to decide.
