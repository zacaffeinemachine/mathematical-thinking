On the 3×3 board the knights were beads on a closed string: they could
slide around the cycle, but they could never pass each other. No
sequence of moves could ever swap the colours.

The culprit was not the board — it was the *graph*. The knight-move
graph on a 3×3 happens to be a single cycle, and cycles are mean.

So what happens on a different board, with a different graph?

Here is a ten-square board, irregular in shape. Two red knights sit at
the top of the board; two blue knights sit at the bottom. The task is
the same: swap the colours, one knight-jump at a time, with no two
knights ever on the same square.

Take a good look at the graph. It is not a cycle this time. It is a
**tree** — three branches all meeting at square 8. One branch is a
single square (8–3). Another is three squares long (8–1–6–10). The
third stretches five squares out (8–7–2–9–4–5), and is completely
empty at the start.

That empty long branch is the whole point. Whenever two knights need
to pass each other at the hub, one of them can duck into the long
branch, wait while the other crosses, and step back out. A parking
lot, in other words. The rigid order that trapped the classical
knights is simply gone.

If you can find a sequence that works, you will have discovered on
your own the same idea that the chapter develops next: that two
puzzles which look almost identical on the surface can live or die by
the shape of the graph underneath.
