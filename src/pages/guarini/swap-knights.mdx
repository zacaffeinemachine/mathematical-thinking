Here is a version of the puzzle Guarini posed in 1512. We have written a
number in the corner of every square so we can talk about them without
pointing.

Two **red** knights sit on one diagonal of a 3×3 board; two **blue**
knights sit on the other. Your task is to move them, one knight-jump at
a time, until the red knights occupy the squares where the blue knights
started, and the blue knights occupy the squares where the red knights
started.

The rules are simple:

- Click a knight to select it; its legal jumps light up.
- Click a highlighted square to jump there.
- A knight cannot land on a square already occupied by another knight.
- Colours must swap — it is not enough to have *some* knight on each
  corner.

It looks, at first, as though the knights must collide — there are four of
them on only nine squares, and the centre square seems to block every
route. Try it for a while before reading on. The numbers in the corners
are there so you can keep track of where each knight has been.

Once you have found a sequence of moves that works (or convinced yourself
that you cannot), pause and ask: *why* did that sequence work? Was there
something special about the order in which the knights moved, or could
any order have worked? Is there a shortest solution, and how would you
know you had found it?

These are the questions that will lead us, in the next pages, to the
idea that turns this puzzle from a fiddly search into something you can
*see* at a glance.
