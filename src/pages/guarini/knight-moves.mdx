Before we meet Guarini's puzzle itself, we need to get comfortable
with the piece that stars in it: the **knight**.

A knight jumps in an **L-shape** — two squares in one direction,
then one square at a right angle. It is the only chess piece that
can hop over others, but here we will only care about where it
lands, not what it jumps over.

Below is a series of small boards. Your job is to guide the knight
to the apple. Click the knight to toggle its highlighted legal
moves; click any highlighted square to jump there. Some boards have
**missing squares** (literal holes in the board), and some have
**forbidden squares** marked with a skull — the knight cannot land
on those either.

Take a moment to notice a few things as you play. The knight is
fussy: from some squares it has up to eight choices, from others
only two. Corners starve it. The same destination might be one
move away on a large board and impossible on a small one. The
*shape* of the board — not just the rules of the knight — is part
of the puzzle.

Keep that observation in mind. It is the first hint of an idea we
will develop carefully in the pages to come.
