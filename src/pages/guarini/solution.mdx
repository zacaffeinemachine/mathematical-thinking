The puzzle felt hard on the board and trivial on the graph. What
changed? Nothing about the rules — only the *picture*.

The board encourages you to think geometrically: rows, columns,
diagonals, the centre. But the rules of the puzzle — knight moves —
do not respect that geometry. Two squares that look far apart (like
the top-left and bottom-right centre edge) may be one knight-move
away, while two squares that look adjacent are not connected by any
single knight move at all. The board *hides* what matters.

The graph throws away the board and keeps only what the rules care
about: which squares can reach which. When we do this, a secret
falls out — the eight squares form a single ring. Problems that are
tangled on the board untangle on the ring.

This is the first habit of a mathematician:

> When a problem feels hard, suspect the representation before you
> suspect the problem. A good re-drawing can turn a maze into a line.

In later chapters we will see this again and again. Logical puzzles
become truth tables. Counting problems become permutations. A long
argument becomes a short one once you find the right object to
attach it to.

Guarini's puzzle is the overture — a five-hundred-year-old warm-up
for the habits we are about to build.
