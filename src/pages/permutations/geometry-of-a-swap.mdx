The simplest permutation that does anything at all is a **swap**:
it exchanges the gems sitting in two chosen slots and leaves every
other gem where it was. In the cycle-decomposition language of the
previous page, this is a permutation whose digraph has exactly one
cycle of length two and all remaining cycles of length one. If the
two swapped slots are $a$ and $b$, we shall write the swap as
$(a,\ b)$ — a single two-cycle, with all the fixed points silently
understood.

A swap is a tiny disturbance: only two slots are directly affected.
What happens if we apply such a tiny disturbance *after* some
larger permutation $\sigma$? The answer, remarkably, is that only
two things are ever possible: either a single cycle of $\sigma$
gets snipped into two smaller cycles, or two separate cycles of
$\sigma$ get stitched together into one bigger cycle. Which of the
two happens is decided by a single question: *do the swapped slots
live in the same cycle of $\sigma$, or in two different cycles?*

## Fission: splitting a cycle

Let $\sigma$ be the permutation on ten slots with cycle
decomposition

$$
\sigma = (10,\ 8,\ 4,\ 7,\ 6,\ 5)\,(1,\ 3,\ 2,\ 9),
$$

and let $\theta$ be the swap $(4,\ 5)$. The two slots being
swapped, $4$ and $5$, both lie in the same cycle of $\sigma$ — the
six-slot ring on the left.

<PermutationDigraph cycles={[[10,8,4,7,6,5],[1,3,2,9]]} caption="Digraph of σ. Slots 4 and 5 sit in the same six-cycle." />

A direct calculation (follow each arrow of $\sigma$ and then apply
$\theta$) gives

$$
\sigma\text{ then }\theta = (10,\ 8,\ 5)\,(4,\ 7,\ 6)\,(1,\ 3,\ 2,\ 9).
$$

<PermutationDigraph cycles={[[10,8,5],[4,7,6],[1,3,2,9]]} caption='Digraph of "σ then θ". The six-cycle has split into two three-cycles.' />

The four-cycle $(1,\ 3,\ 2,\ 9)$ on the right is untouched — slots
$4$ and $5$ never enter it. The interesting thing is what happened
to the long ring: it has broken cleanly into two smaller rings, of
three slots each.

*Why does the ring split?* Look at the two arrows of $\sigma$ that
*enter* slots $4$ and $5$. There is exactly one such arrow into
each, because every slot of a permutation receives exactly one
arrow. The arrow into $4$ starts at slot $8$ (since $\sigma$ sends
$8$ to $4$), and the arrow into $5$ starts at slot $6$ (since
$\sigma$ sends $6$ to $5$).

Now consider what happens when we perform $\theta$ right after
$\sigma$. For a slot $i$ that $\sigma$ sends to neither $4$ nor
$5$, the swap $\theta$ leaves $\sigma(i)$ untouched, so the arrow
out of $i$ is unchanged. The only arrows that get redirected are
the two whose heads landed on $4$ or $5$:

- The arrow from slot $8$, which used to point at $4$, now points
  at $5$ (because $\theta$ sends $4$ to $5$).
- The arrow from slot $6$, which used to point at $5$, now points
  at $4$.

In other words, composing with $\theta$ has the sole effect of
*swapping the heads of these two incoming arrows*. Everything
else in the digraph is untouched.

This small rewiring is enough to split the ring. Starting from
$10$ and following the new arrows, we walk
$10 \to 8 \to 5 \to 10$ — a three-cycle. Starting from $4$, we
walk $4 \to 7 \to 6 \to 4$ — a second three-cycle. The original
six-cycle has been snipped into two halves precisely at the
rewiring site.

## Fusion: merging two cycles

Now let $\sigma$ be the permutation on ten slots with cycle
decomposition

$$
\sigma = (3,\ 8,\ 2,\ 6)\,(1,\ 4,\ 5)\,(7,\ 10)\,(9),
$$

and let $\theta$ be the swap $(3,\ 4)$. This time the two slots
being swapped live in *different* cycles of $\sigma$: slot $3$
sits in the four-cycle on the left, and slot $4$ sits in the
three-cycle beside it.

<PermutationDigraph cycles={[[3,8,2,6],[1,4,5],[7,10],[9]]} caption="Digraph of σ. Slots 3 and 4 sit in two different cycles." />

A short calculation shows that

$$
\sigma\text{ then }\theta = (1,\ 3,\ 8,\ 2,\ 6,\ 4,\ 5)\,(7,\ 10)\,(9).
$$

<PermutationDigraph cycles={[[1,3,8,2,6,4,5],[7,10],[9]]} caption='Digraph of "σ then θ". The two cycles containing 3 and 4 have fused into one seven-cycle.' />

The cycles $(7,\ 10)$ and $(9)$ are carried along unchanged. But
the two cycles that contained $3$ and $4$ have merged into a
single seven-slot ring.

*Why do the two cycles merge?* The reasoning is the mirror image
of the splitting argument. In $\sigma$ the arrow entering $3$
starts at slot $6$, and the arrow entering $4$ starts at slot $1$.
Performing $\theta$ afterwards swaps the heads of these two
arrows: the arrow from $6$ now points at $4$ instead of $3$, and
the arrow from $1$ now points at $3$ instead of $4$. No other
arrow is touched.

This single rewiring stitches the two separate rings together.
Starting from $1$ and following the new arrows, we now walk

$$
1 \to 3 \to 8 \to 2 \to 6 \to 4 \to 5 \to 1,
$$

a single seven-cycle that passes through every slot of both
original rings. Two cycles have become one.

## The general picture

The two examples are instances of a single general fact.

  borderRadius:4,

Let $\sigma$ be any permutation on $n$ slots and let $\theta$ be
the swap $(a,\ b)$.

- If slots $a$ and $b$ lie in the *same* cycle of $\sigma$, then
  the number of cycles of "$\sigma$ then $\theta$" is one *more*
  than the number of cycles of $\sigma$: that cycle has split in
  two.
- If slots $a$ and $b$ lie in *different* cycles of $\sigma$, then
  the number of cycles of "$\sigma$ then $\theta$" is one *less*
  than the number of cycles of $\sigma$: those two cycles have
  fused into one.

</div>

The argument we used in both examples works word for word in
general. In the digraph of $\sigma$, composing with the swap
$(a,\ b)$ has the sole effect of exchanging the heads of the two
arrows that enter $a$ and $b$; every other arrow is left alone.
A single such rewiring is always exactly enough either to snip
one ring into two or to stitch two rings into one — no other
outcome is possible.

In either case, *the number of cycles changes by exactly one*.
This tiny accounting fact will turn out to be the key to
everything that comes next.
