We have examined in detail how a single swap acts on the sign: it
flips it. What happens when we compose two *arbitrary*
permutations, neither of which is necessarily a swap? The answer,
as one might suspect by now, is very clean. The sign of the
composition is nothing more than the Boolean sum of the individual
signs — so the Boolean addition table we defined earlier was no
idle invention, but exactly the rule needed to combine signs.

## Theorem

<div style={{
  margin:'20px 0',
  padding:'14px 18px',
  borderLeft:'4px solid var(--accent)',
  background:'var(--surface)',
  borderRadius:4,
}}>

Let $\sigma$ and $\tau$ be permutations of $n$ objects. Then

$$
\mathrm{sgn}(\sigma\text{ then }\tau) = \mathrm{sgn}(\sigma) + \mathrm{sgn}(\tau).
$$

</div>

*Proof.* Every permutation can be written as a sequence of swaps.
Write

$$
\sigma = \alpha_1\text{ then }\cdots\text{ then }\alpha_k,
$$

a sequence of $k$ swaps, and

$$
\tau = \beta_1\text{ then }\cdots\text{ then }\beta_m,
$$

a sequence of $m$ swaps. Concatenating, "$\sigma$ then $\tau$" is
itself a sequence of $k + m$ swaps. The sign-of-a-swap-sequence
identity from the previous page gives

$$
\mathrm{sgn}(\sigma) = \psi(k),\qquad \mathrm{sgn}(\tau) = \psi(m),\qquad \mathrm{sgn}(\sigma\text{ then }\tau) = \psi(k + m).
$$

Since $\psi$ respects addition, $\psi(k + m) = \psi(k) + \psi(m)$,
which is exactly $\mathrm{sgn}(\sigma\text{ then }\tau) = \mathrm{sgn}(\sigma) + \mathrm{sgn}(\tau)$.
∎

## In plain words

Unpacking the theorem in the language of even and odd
permutations, the four possibilities are:

- Even then even is even.
- Even then odd is odd.
- Odd then even is odd.
- Odd then odd is even.

These are just the four lines of the Boolean addition table in
disguise. In particular, performing any even permutation changes
nothing about the parity, while performing any odd permutation
flips it — a clean generalisation of what a single swap does, from
a single transposition to *any* odd permutation at all.

}}>Exercise 1 — Two permutations of fifteen</div>

Let $\sigma$ be the permutation of $15$ objects

$$
\begin{aligned}
&1 \mapsto 13,\quad 2 \mapsto 2,\quad 3 \mapsto 15,\quad 4 \mapsto 14,\quad 5 \mapsto 10,\\
&6 \mapsto 6,\quad 7 \mapsto 12,\quad 8 \mapsto 3,\quad 9 \mapsto 4,\quad 10 \mapsto 1,\\
&11 \mapsto 7,\quad 12 \mapsto 9,\quad 13 \mapsto 5,\quad 14 \mapsto 11,\quad 15 \mapsto 8,
\end{aligned}
$$

and let $\tau$ be the permutation of $15$ objects

$$
\begin{aligned}
&1 \mapsto 14,\quad 2 \mapsto 9,\quad 3 \mapsto 10,\quad 4 \mapsto 2,\quad 5 \mapsto 12,\\
&6 \mapsto 6,\quad 7 \mapsto 5,\quad 8 \mapsto 11,\quad 9 \mapsto 15,\quad 10 \mapsto 3,\\
&11 \mapsto 8,\quad 12 \mapsto 7,\quad 13 \mapsto 4,\quad 14 \mapsto 1,\quad 15 \mapsto 13.
\end{aligned}
$$

**(a)** Find the stupid numbers and signs of each of the six
permutations $\sigma$, $\tau$, "$\sigma$ then $\sigma$", "$\tau$
then $\sigma$", "$\sigma$ then $\tau$", and "$\sigma$ then $\tau$
then $\tau$".

**(b)** Can $\sigma$ be expressed as a sequence of an even number
of swaps? An odd number of swaps?

**(c)** Same question for $\tau$.
</div>

Let $\sigma$ be the permutation of $10$ objects

$$
\begin{aligned}
&1 \mapsto 8,\quad 2 \mapsto 9,\quad 3 \mapsto 10,\quad 4 \mapsto 2,\quad 5 \mapsto 7,\\
&6 \mapsto 5,\quad 7 \mapsto 3,\quad 8 \mapsto 1,\quad 9 \mapsto 4,\quad 10 \mapsto 6,
\end{aligned}
$$

and let $\tau$ be the permutation of $10$ objects

$$
\begin{aligned}
&1 \mapsto 7,\quad 2 \mapsto 6,\quad 3 \mapsto 9,\quad 4 \mapsto 8,\quad 5 \mapsto 10,\\
&6 \mapsto 1,\quad 7 \mapsto 3,\quad 8 \mapsto 2,\quad 9 \mapsto 5,\quad 10 \mapsto 4.
\end{aligned}
$$

**(a)** Draw the digraphs of $\sigma$ and $\tau$.

**(b)** Find the stupid numbers and signs of $\sigma$ and $\tau$.

**(c)** Draw the digraph of "$\sigma$ then $\tau$" and find its
sign.

**(d)** For each of $\sigma$, $\tau$, and "$\sigma$ then $\tau$",
decide whether it can be written as a sequence of an even number
of swaps, and whether it can be written as a sequence of an odd
number of swaps.
