In the previous page we noticed that applying a single swap to a
permutation changes its cycle count by exactly one — never by zero,
never by two. It will be convenient to repackage this observation
in terms of a single number attached to every permutation, a
number that ticks up or down by one each time a swap is applied.

## Stupid number of a permutation

Let $\sigma$ be a permutation of $n$ objects, and let $\ell$ denote
the number of cycles in its cycle decomposition. The **stupid
number** of $\sigma$ is defined to be

$$
\mathrm{st}(\sigma) = n - \ell.
$$

In words: count the slots, count the cycles, and subtract. The
name is cheerfully informal, but the quantity itself is far from
stupid — it will turn out to be the yardstick by which we measure
how much "work" a permutation represents. Since every cycle takes
up at least one slot, $\ell$ can never exceed $n$, and it is
always at least $1$ (unless $n = 0$). So

$$
0 \;\leq\; \mathrm{st}(\sigma) \;\leq\; n - 1.
$$

Three worked examples, from tame to wild:

- *The do-nothing permutation.* Let $\sigma$ leave every gem in
  place. Every slot is its own little ring, so $\ell = n$, and
  $\mathrm{st}(\sigma) = n - n = 0$. This is the only permutation
  whose stupid number is $0$.
- *A swap.* Let $\sigma$ be the swap of two slots $a$ and $b$.
  Its cycle decomposition contains one two-cycle together with
  $n - 2$ fixed points, for $\ell = n - 1$. So
  $\mathrm{st}(\sigma) = n - (n - 1) = 1$. Every swap has stupid
  number $1$.
- *A mixed example.* For
  $\sigma = (1,\ 3,\ 8,\ 2,\ 6,\ 4,\ 5)\,(7,\ 10)\,(9)$ on ten
  slots, $\ell = 3$, so $\mathrm{st}(\sigma) = 10 - 3 = 7$.

## A swap changes the stupid number by exactly one

Let $\sigma$ be any permutation of $n$ objects and let $\theta$ be
the swap $(a,\ b)$. The previous page described precisely how the
cycle count of $\sigma$ responds when $\theta$ is applied
afterwards — and since the stupid number is just $n$ minus the
cycle count, the two move in opposite directions.

<div style={{
  margin:'20px 0',
  padding:'14px 18px',
  borderLeft:'4px solid var(--accent)',
  background:'var(--surface)',
  borderRadius:4,
}}>

$$
\mathrm{st}(\sigma\text{ then }\theta) \;=\; \mathrm{st}(\sigma) \,\pm\, 1.
$$

More precisely:

- If slots $a$ and $b$ lie in the *same* cycle of $\sigma$, that
  cycle splits in two. The cycle count grows by one, so the stupid
  number *drops* by one.
- If slots $a$ and $b$ lie in *different* cycles of $\sigma$, those
  two cycles fuse into one. The cycle count shrinks by one, so the
  stupid number *grows* by one.

</div>

Whichever of the two happens, the stupid number shifts by a single
unit — up or down, but never by zero and never by two. This steady
one-step-at-a-time behaviour is exactly what will let us keep
track of long sequences of swaps in the sections to come.

## Boolean addition

For many purposes, the precise value of the stupid number carries
more information than we actually need: what really matters is
only whether it is *even* or *odd*. Collapsing the stupid number
down to its parity yields a cruder — but remarkably useful —
invariant of a permutation, which we shall call its **sign**.
Before we define it, we pause to introduce a small piece of
arithmetic on a two-element alphabet.

Let $\mathsf{odd}$ and $\mathsf{even}$ be two formal symbols. We
equip them with the addition rule

- $\mathsf{odd} + \mathsf{odd} = \mathsf{even}$;
- $\mathsf{odd} + \mathsf{even} = \mathsf{odd}$;
- $\mathsf{even} + \mathsf{odd} = \mathsf{odd}$;
- $\mathsf{even} + \mathsf{even} = \mathsf{even}$.

The inspiration is clear: odd plus odd is even, odd plus even is
odd, and so on — exactly the rules for combining parities of
integers. We shall refer to these four laws collectively as
**Boolean addition**. Note that $\mathsf{even}$ plays the role of
zero (adding it changes nothing) and $\mathsf{odd}$ is its own
negative.

## Sign of a permutation

For each integer $n$, let $\psi(n) = \mathsf{odd}$ if $n$ is odd
and $\psi(n) = \mathsf{even}$ if $n$ is even. Boolean addition
was cooked up precisely so that $\psi(a + b) = \psi(a) + \psi(b)$
for all integers $a, b$.

The **sign** of a permutation $\sigma$ is the parity of its stupid
number:

$$
\mathrm{sgn}(\sigma) = \psi(\mathrm{st}(\sigma)).
$$

We call $\sigma$ an **even** permutation if
$\mathrm{sgn}(\sigma) = \mathsf{even}$ and an **odd** permutation
if $\mathrm{sgn}(\sigma) = \mathsf{odd}$. Examples:

- The do-nothing permutation has stupid number $0$, which is
  even — sign $\mathsf{even}$.
- Any swap has stupid number $1$, which is odd — sign
  $\mathsf{odd}$.
- The ten-slot example with stupid number $7$ has sign
  $\mathsf{odd}$.

## Effect of a swap on the sign

Let $\sigma$ be any permutation and let $\theta$ be a swap. The
stupid number of "$\sigma$ then $\theta$" differs from that of
$\sigma$ by exactly $\pm 1$, so the two have *opposite* parities.
Translating into signs:

<div style={{
  margin:'20px 0',
  padding:'14px 18px',
  borderLeft:'4px solid var(--accent)',
  background:'var(--surface)',
  borderRadius:4,
}}>

$$
\mathrm{sgn}(\sigma\text{ then }\theta) = \mathrm{sgn}(\sigma) + \mathsf{odd}.
$$

</div>

In plain English: composing with a single swap *flips* the sign —
an even permutation becomes odd, and an odd permutation becomes
even.

Let $k \geq 2$ be an integer and let $n \geq k$. A permutation
$\sigma$ of $n$ objects is called a **$k$-cycle** if in the
digraph of $\sigma$ there is exactly one cycle featuring $k$ slots,
and every other slot is a fixed point. (For example, a swap is
nothing but a $2$-cycle.)

**(a)** Determine the sign of every $5$-cycle. Does the answer
depend on $n$?

**(b)** Determine the sign of every $6$-cycle. Does the answer
depend on $n$?

**(c)** More generally, give a rule that expresses the sign of a
$k$-cycle in terms of $k$ alone.
