The 15 puzzle was not the only mass-market toy whose secret turned
out to be the sign of a permutation. Another classic, sold under
the name *TopSpin* and many imitators, lives on an oval track
instead of a square grid. Its rules are different from the 15
puzzle, but the question one ends up asking is the same: out of
the enormous number of arrangements the tokens *look* as if they
could reach, exactly which ones can they actually reach? And once
again the answer is decided, before any case analysis, by parity.

## The puzzle

Imagine an oval track with seven slots evenly spaced around it,
and seven numbered tokens, labelled $1$ through $7$, sitting one
in each slot. The four slots at the top of the oval are special:
they sit inside a small movable cradle that can be lifted, turned
over, and dropped back in. We shall call this cradle the **flip
window**.

The puzzle allows exactly two kinds of move:

- **Rotate.** Push every token one slot clockwise around the
  oval. The token formerly at slot $7$ comes around to slot $1$,
  and every other token shifts one place along.
- **Flip.** Reverse the order of the four tokens currently inside
  the flip window. The token at the leftmost window slot trades
  places with the one at the rightmost, and the two middle tokens
  trade places with each other.

The tokens come scrambled out of the box, and the goal is to
bring them back to the sorted order $1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7$ around
the track using only these two moves.

## The two moves as permutations

The slots are fixed in space; only the tokens move. So each move
is a permutation of the seven tokens, and we can write down the
permutations explicitly.

*The rotate.* Every token moves one step clockwise. In cycle
notation, this is the single seven-cycle

$$
R = (1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7).
$$

*The flip.* The flip leaves slots $5, 6, 7$ alone and reverses
the contents of slots $1, 2, 3, 4$. Slot $1$ trades with slot $4$,
slot $2$ trades with slot $3$. As a product of swaps,

$$
F = (1,\ 4)(2,\ 3).
$$

## Both moves are even

With $R$ and $F$ in hand, we read off their signs.

*Sign of $R$.* $R$ is a single seven-cycle, so its cycle
decomposition has one cycle. With $n = 7$ slots and $\ell = 1$
cycle,

$$
\mathrm{st}(R) = n - \ell = 7 - 1 = 6,
$$

which is even. Therefore $\mathrm{sgn}(R) = \mathsf{even}$.

*Sign of $F$.* $F$ is the product of two disjoint two-cycles
together with three fixed points (slots $5, 6, 7$), for a total
of $\ell = 5$ cycles. So

$$
\mathrm{st}(F) = 7 - 5 = 2,
$$

which is also even. Therefore $\mathrm{sgn}(F) = \mathsf{even}$.

*Both* of the puzzle's moves are even permutations. This is the
only fact about the moves we shall need.

## An unreachable target

Out of the box, suppose the tokens come within one whisker of
being sorted: $1$ and $2$ are in each other's slots, but
everything else is already correct. The starting configuration is
obtained from the solved one by the single swap

$$
\tau = (1,\ 2),
$$

which has $\mathrm{st}(\tau) = 1$ and hence
$\mathrm{sgn}(\tau) = \mathsf{odd}$.

Now suppose, for contradiction, that some sequence of $k$ moves
brings the swapped board back to the sorted one. Each move is
either an $R$ or an $F$, so the overall effect is a composition
of $k$ permutations, each of which is even.

By the sign-of-a-composition rule, the sign of a composition is
the Boolean sum of the signs of its pieces. Each piece contributes
$\mathsf{even}$, and adding $\mathsf{even}$ to itself any number
of times in Boolean addition gives $\mathsf{even}$:

$$
\mathrm{sgn}(\text{$k$ moves}) = \underbrace{\mathsf{even} + \mathsf{even} + \cdots + \mathsf{even}}_{k \text{ times}} = \mathsf{even}.
$$

But the overall effect of the moves is supposed to equal $\tau$
(or its inverse, which has the same sign), so
$\mathrm{sgn}(\tau) = \mathsf{even}$. This contradicts
$\mathrm{sgn}(\tau) = \mathsf{odd}$. The assumed sequence of moves
cannot exist.

The configuration with tokens $1$ and $2$ swapped (and every other

configuration by any sequence of rotates and flips.

## Half of all arrangements are out of reach

The argument we just made did not really lean on the particular
pair $1, 2$; it used only that a single swap is odd. The same
argument therefore rules out *every* odd target:

<div style={{
  margin:'20px 0',
  padding:'14px 18px',
  borderLeft:'4px solid var(--accent)',
  background:'var(--surface)',
  borderRadius:4,
}}>

Starting from the solved configuration, no odd permutation of the
seven tokens is reachable.

</div>

The seven tokens admit $7! = 5040$ arrangements in total, and
exactly half of them — namely the $2520$ even ones — survive the
parity test. The other half are unreachable, no matter how
cleverly one combines rotates and flips.

One could go further and ask whether every *even* permutation is
reachable. The answer turns out to be yes, but the proof requires
more than parity: it needs an honest construction that, given any
even target, exhibits a sequence of moves that achieves it.
Parity alone settles only the negative half of the question —
which arrangements are forbidden — and that is already a striking
amount to learn from a single Boolean addition.

## A note on the commercial puzzle

The toy sold as TopSpin uses twenty tokens around the oval, with
the same flip window of size four. The flip is still a product of
two disjoint swaps — an even permutation — but the rotate is now
a single twenty-cycle, with

$$
\mathrm{st}(R) = 20 - 1 = 19,
$$

an *odd* number. So in the commercial version one move is even
and the other is odd, which means the parity test rules nothing
out: every permutation of the twenty tokens has at least the
right parity to be reachable. (And indeed, with twenty tokens,
every permutation *is* reachable.)

The contrast is instructive. Changing only the number of slots
around the track turns a puzzle whose configuration space is cut
in half by parity into one where parity is silent. The same
simple Boolean addition decides the matter in both cases; it just
happens to give different verdicts.
