Swaps are the simplest non-trivial permutations, and we have
already seen that they pack a surprising punch: a single swap
flips the sign of any permutation. In this section we examine
swaps as building blocks and ask three natural questions. Can
every permutation be realised as a sequence of swaps? If so, must
we allow *every* possible swap, or does a small collection
suffice? And how small can that collection be made before we lose
the ability to realise some permutation?

## Every permutation is a sequence of swaps

<div style={{
  margin:'20px 0',
  padding:'14px 18px',
  borderLeft:'4px solid var(--accent)',
  background:'var(--surface)',
  borderRadius:4,
}}>

Every permutation of $n$ objects can be written as
"$\theta_1$ then $\theta_2$ then $\cdots$ then $\theta_k$" for some
finite list of swaps $\theta_1, \theta_2, \ldots, \theta_k$.

</div>

The recipe is to chop $\sigma$ into cycles and handle each cycle
separately. Suppose $\sigma$ has a cycle
$(a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_m)$ — so $\sigma$ sends $a_1$ to $a_2$,
$a_2$ to $a_3$, and so on, with $a_m$ sent back to $a_1$. This
single cycle is the same as the sequence of $m - 1$ swaps

$$
(a_1,\ a_2)\text{ then }(a_1,\ a_3)\text{ then }\cdots\text{ then }(a_1,\ a_m).
$$

For example, the three-cycle $(1, 2, 3)$ is the same as
"$(1,\ 2)$ then $(1,\ 3)$": tracing each gem, $1$ goes to $2$ and
stays there, $2$ goes to $1$ then on to $3$, and $3$ stays put
then jumps to $1$ — exactly the cycle $1 \to 2 \to 3 \to 1$.

Applying this expansion to each cycle of $\sigma$ in turn (and
recalling that different cycles share no slots, so their swaps do
not interfere with one another) expresses $\sigma$ as a single
long sequence of swaps. The total number of swaps used is

$$
(m_1 - 1) + (m_2 - 1) + \cdots + (m_\ell - 1) \;=\; n - \ell \;=\; \mathrm{st}(\sigma),
$$

where $m_1, \ldots, m_\ell$ are the lengths of the $\ell$ cycles
of $\sigma$. So every permutation can be built using exactly
$\mathrm{st}(\sigma)$ swaps by this recipe — a pleasant fact that
explains, at last, why "stupid number" was a reasonable thing to
define.

## A consequence for the sign

The recipe above produces *one* expression of $\sigma$ as a
sequence of swaps. In general there are many others. Any such
expression, however, is forced to have a particular parity.
Suppose $\sigma$ is written as a sequence of $k$ swaps,

$$
\sigma = \theta_1\text{ then }\theta_2\text{ then }\cdots\text{ then }\theta_k.
$$

Starting from the do-nothing permutation (whose sign is
$\mathsf{even}$) and composing with one swap at a time, the sign
flips $k$ times along the way. Therefore

$$
\mathrm{sgn}(\sigma) = \psi(k).
$$

In particular, **any two realisations of the same permutation as
sequences of swaps must use the same parity of swaps** — an even
permutation requires an even number of swaps every time, and an
odd permutation requires an odd number every time, no matter how
we slice it.

## Star swaps suffice

The recipe above helps itself to arbitrary swaps. But in fact a
very restricted collection is already enough to realise every
permutation. Call the $n - 1$ swaps

$$
(1,\ 2),\quad (1,\ 3),\quad (1,\ 4),\quad \ldots,\quad (1,\ n)
$$

the **star swaps**. (The name comes from the picture: joining
slot $1$ to every other slot by an edge produces a star with slot
$1$ at its centre.)

<div style={{
  margin:'20px 0',
  padding:'14px 18px',
  borderLeft:'4px solid var(--accent)',
  background:'var(--surface)',
  borderRadius:4,
}}>

Every permutation of $n$ objects can be built using only star swaps.

</div>

The reason is that any swap whatsoever can itself be written as a
short sequence of star swaps. Let $(i,\ j)$ be a swap with neither
$i$ nor $j$ equal to $1$. A direct check — track what each of the
three swaps below does to the slots $1$, $i$, $j$ — confirms the
identity

$$
(i,\ j) \;=\; (1,\ i)\text{ then }(1,\ j)\text{ then }(1,\ i).
$$

So each non-star swap expands into three star swaps. Taking the
sequence produced by the previous recipe and replacing every
non-star swap by its three-swap expansion yields a realisation of
$\sigma$ using only star swaps.

## Can we do with fewer than $n - 1$ swaps?

We have just seen that a collection of $n - 1$ swaps (the star
swaps) is enough. Is there an even smaller collection — say of
only $n - 2$ swaps — that still does the job?

<div style={{
  margin:'20px 0',
  padding:'14px 18px',
  borderLeft:'4px solid var(--accent)',
  background:'var(--surface)',
  borderRadius:4,
}}>

No collection of $n - 2$ swaps can realise every permutation of
$n$ objects.

</div>

Let $S$ be any collection of $n - 2$ swaps. Build a graph $G$ on
the vertex set $\{1, 2, \ldots, n\}$ by joining vertices $i$ and
$j$ with an edge whenever the swap $(i,\ j)$ belongs to $S$. The
graph $G$ has only $n - 2$ edges, but a connected graph on $n$
vertices needs at least $n - 1$ edges. So $G$ is disconnected, and
its vertex set splits into two or more components.

Now pick any two slots $i$ and $j$ that lie in *different*
components of $G$. Any single swap drawn from $S$ only exchanges
two slots joined by an edge of $G$, and so can move a gem only
between vertices in the same component. Chaining such swaps
together extends the reach only along edge-paths in $G$, and the
components of $G$ are never crossed. Consequently no sequence of
swaps from $S$ can send the gem at slot $i$ to slot $j$, which
means the swap $(i,\ j)$ itself cannot be realised using only the
swaps in $S$.
