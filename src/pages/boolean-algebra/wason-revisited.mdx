Recall the Wason puzzle from the chapter on Logical Thinking. Four
cards lie on a table, each with a colour on one side (red or blue)
and a digit on the other ($4$ or $7$). The claim under test is

<div style={{textAlign:'center', fontStyle:'italic', margin:'12px 0'}}>
"If a card is red on one side, then it has an even number on the other side."
</div>

The question is which cards we must turn over to test the claim.

## Setting up the equation

Label the four cards $A, B, C, D$, and to each card $X$ attach a
pair of yes/no values

$$
x_r = \texttt{1} \text{ if $X$ is red on its colour side}, \qquad x_e = \texttt{1} \text{ if $X$ is even on its digit side.}
$$

The claim, applied to a single card $X$, has the dictionary form
"if $x_r$ is yes, then $x_e$ is yes," which translates to the
equation

$$
x_r \cdot (\texttt{1} + x_e) = \texttt{0}.
$$

The card $X$ violates the claim exactly when
$x_r(\texttt{1} + x_e) = \texttt{1}$. For each card, one of
$x_r, x_e$ is visible and the other is hidden. The card needs to be
turned over precisely when the value of $x_r(\texttt{1} + x_e)$
*depends* on the hidden value: only then could the back of the
card change the answer.

## Computing for each card

For a generic card $X$, call $x_r(\texttt{1} + x_e)$ the
**violation indicator** of $X$: it equals $\texttt{1}$ exactly when
$X$ violates the claim, and $\texttt{0}$ otherwise.

<div style={{
  margin:'16px 0',
  padding:'14px 18px',
  border:'1px solid var(--rule)',
  borderLeft:'4px solid var(--accent)',
  borderRadius:4,
  background:'var(--surface)',
}}>

**The red card.** Here $x_r = \texttt{1}$ is known. The violation
indicator becomes $\texttt{1} \cdot (\texttt{1} + x_e) = \texttt{1} + x_e$.
Its value still depends on the hidden $x_e$, so we cannot tell
from the visible side alone whether the card violates the claim.
**Must flip.**

</div>

<div style={{
  margin:'16px 0',
  padding:'14px 18px',
  border:'1px solid var(--rule)',
  borderLeft:'4px solid var(--muted)',
  borderRadius:4,
  background:'var(--surface)',
}}>

**The blue card.** Here $x_r = \texttt{0}$ is known. The violation
indicator becomes $\texttt{0} \cdot (\texttt{1} + x_e) = \texttt{0}$,
regardless of $x_e$. The visible side already settles the matter
— the card cannot violate the claim. **No need to flip.**

</div>

<div style={{
  margin:'16px 0',
  padding:'14px 18px',
  border:'1px solid var(--rule)',
  borderLeft:'4px solid var(--muted)',
  borderRadius:4,
  background:'var(--surface)',
}}>

**The 4 card.** Here $x_e = \texttt{1}$ is known. Then
$\texttt{1} + x_e = \texttt{0}$, so the violation indicator becomes
$x_r \cdot \texttt{0} = \texttt{0}$, regardless of $x_r$. Again the
visible side settles the matter. **No need to flip.**

</div>

<div style={{
  margin:'16px 0',
  padding:'14px 18px',
  border:'1px solid var(--rule)',
  borderLeft:'4px solid var(--accent)',
  borderRadius:4,
  background:'var(--surface)',
}}>

**The 7 card.** Here $x_e = \texttt{0}$ is known. Then
$\texttt{1} + x_e = \texttt{1}$, so the violation indicator becomes
$x_r \cdot \texttt{1} = x_r$. Its value depends on the hidden
$x_r$, so the visible side does not tell us whether the card
violates the claim. **Must flip.**

</div>

The two cards we must turn over are the **red card** and the
**7 card**. We have re-derived the answer of the original chapter,
but here it has dropped out of arithmetic — one short calculation
per card, with no prose case analysis.

The pattern generalises. Whenever a yes/no claim translates to an
equation of the form $E = \texttt{0}$, deciding *which inputs
matter* reduces to asking, for each input variable in turn:
**does the value of $E$ still depend on this variable once the
known values are plugged in?** If yes, that variable's hidden
value matters; if no, it does not. The algebra makes the question
mechanical.
