## Everything you would hope for, holds

Here is a short list of properties of $+$ and $\cdot$. Each one can
be checked by writing down the four (or eight) rows of a tiny table
and comparing the two columns.

  background:'var(--surface)',

For all values $a, b, c$ in $\{\texttt{0}, \texttt{1}\}$:

- *Order does not matter*: $a + b = b + a$ and $a \cdot b = b \cdot a$.
- *Grouping does not matter*: $(a + b) + c = a + (b + c)$ and $(a \cdot b) \cdot c = a \cdot (b \cdot c)$.
- *Multiplication shares over addition*: $a \cdot (b + c) = a \cdot b + a \cdot c$.
- *Identities*: $a + \texttt{0} = a$ and $a \cdot \texttt{1} = a$.
- *Annihilator*: $a \cdot \texttt{0} = \texttt{0}$.

</div>

None of these are surprising. They are exactly the properties you
would expect of any arithmetic worth the name. The reason they hold
here, free of charge, is the parity reading from the previous page:
each of these laws is true for ordinary integers, and reading every
integer "through the parity lens" preserves the law.

## Two laws that have no analogue in ordinary arithmetic

Two further identities are special to the two-element world. They
look strange at first sight, and they are the source of most of the
simplifying we will do in this chapter.

**(a) Adding a value to itself gives zero.** For every value $a$ in
$\{\texttt{0}, \texttt{1}\}$:

$$
a + a = \texttt{0}.
$$

Check: $\texttt{0} + \texttt{0} = \texttt{0}$ and
$\texttt{1} + \texttt{1} = \texttt{0}$. (Through the parity lens,
"odd plus odd is even" is exactly this fact.) A useful rephrasing:
subtraction in this world is the same as addition. There is no need
to ever write a minus sign — if you wanted to compute "$b$ minus
$a$," just write $b + a$ instead. In particular, $\texttt{1} - a$
and $\texttt{1} + a$ mean the same thing, and from now on we write
$\texttt{1} + a$ for "the flip of $a$."

**(b) Squaring a value gives back the value.** For every $a$ in
$\{\texttt{0}, \texttt{1}\}$:

$$
a \cdot a = a, \qquad \text{written more compactly as } a^2 = a.
$$

Check: $\texttt{0} \cdot \texttt{0} = \texttt{0}$ and
$\texttt{1} \cdot \texttt{1} = \texttt{1}$. So no power of $a$ above
the first ever needs to appear: $a^3 = a^2 \cdot a = a \cdot a = a$,
and so on. Every polynomial we ever write in boolean variables can
be reduced, by repeated use of $a^2 = a$, to one with no exponents
at all.

## A worked simplification

To see the new laws in action, simplify

$$
E = (a + b)^2 + a + b.
$$

Expand the square by the distributive law:

$$
(a + b)^2 = (a + b)(a + b) = a^2 + ab + ba + b^2.
$$

Now $a^2 = a$, $b^2 = b$, and $ab + ab = \texttt{0}$, so

$$
(a + b)^2 = a + \texttt{0} + b = a + b.
$$

Therefore $E = (a + b) + (a + b) = \texttt{0}$, again by "a thing
plus itself is zero." The expression is identically zero. Notice
how cheap the steps were: *things added to themselves vanish*,
*squares collapse*. Once these reflexes settle in, expressions get
much smaller very quickly.

## More worked simplifications

Four more tiny examples. Each one leans on the same two moves.

**A value times its flip.** $\;a(\texttt{1} + a) = a + a^2 = a + a = \texttt{0}.\;$
Under the yes/no reading this is common sense: "$a$ is yes *and*
$a$ is no" cannot happen.

**The freshman's dream, for real this time.** In ordinary algebra,
beginners often write $(a + b + c)^2 = a^2 + b^2 + c^2$ and lose
marks for it. In the boolean world that mistake becomes a theorem:

$$
(a + b + c)^2 = a^2 + b^2 + c^2 + 2ab + 2ac + 2bc.
$$

Every cross term is doubled, and $2xy = xy + xy = \texttt{0}$, so
all three cross terms vanish. Using $a^2 = a$ etc., we get
$(a + b + c)^2 = a + b + c$. The same argument works for any number
of terms: *every boolean expression squares to itself*.

**A symmetric expression that is always zero.**

$$
a(b + c) + b(c + a) + c(a + b) = ab + ac + bc + ab + ac + bc = \texttt{0}.
$$

**A product of three sums that collapses.**

$$
(a + b)(b + c)(a + c) = \texttt{0}.
$$

Among three boolean values at least two must agree — there are only
two values to go around — so at least one of the three sums is
already $\texttt{0}$, and a product with a $\texttt{0}$ factor is
$\texttt{0}$.
