Now we put on the "yes/no" reading and use the algebra to talk
about logical conditions. Each yes/no condition will translate into
an arithmetic equation that holds exactly when the condition holds.
Once a condition has become an equation, we are free to manipulate
it with the laws on the previous page.

## Both yes / at least one no

The multiplication table tells us at once that

$$
a \cdot b = \texttt{1} \quad \text{means} \quad \text{``both $a$ and $b$ are yes,''}
$$

$$
a \cdot b = \texttt{0} \quad \text{means} \quad \text{``at least one of $a$ and $b$ is no.''}
$$

The same pattern extends: $abc = \texttt{1}$ says "all three are
yes," and $abc = \texttt{0}$ says "at least one of the three is no."

## Both no / at least one yes

Apply the same trick to the flipped variables $\texttt{1} + a$ and
$\texttt{1} + b$. Their product equals $\texttt{1}$ exactly when
both flips are yes — which says both $a$ and $b$ are no:

$$
(\texttt{1} + a)(\texttt{1} + b) = \texttt{1} \;\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\; \text{both are no},
$$

$$
(\texttt{1} + a)(\texttt{1} + b) = \texttt{0} \;\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\; \text{at least one is yes}.
$$

Expanding the product, "at least one yes" has the equational form
$a + b + ab = \texttt{1}$.

## Exactly one / agree

The boolean addition table is itself a translator. Reading the
table:

- $a + b = \texttt{1}$ on rows where $a$ and $b$ disagree.
- $a + b = \texttt{0}$ on rows where $a$ and $b$ agree.

So $a + b = \texttt{1}$ means "exactly one of $a, b$ is yes," and
$a + b = \texttt{0}$ means "$a$ and $b$ agree." Boolean addition is,
in fact, the same operation engineers call *xor* — the disagreement
detector.

## "If yes, then yes"

A claim of the form "if $a$ is yes, then $b$ is yes" fails exactly
when $a$ is yes and $b$ is no — that is, when $a = \texttt{1}$ and
$\texttt{1} + b = \texttt{1}$. Both have to be yes simultaneously,
so the failure pattern is $a(\texttt{1} + b) = \texttt{1}$. The
claim itself is therefore the equation

$$
a(\texttt{1} + b) = \texttt{0} \quad \text{means} \quad \text{``if $a$ is yes, then $b$ is yes.''}
$$

$\texttt{0}$ everywhere else?*

## Joining conditions

Suppose we have two conditions, each of the form $E_1 = \texttt{0}$
and $E_2 = \texttt{0}$, and we want to express "both" or "at least
one" as a single equation.

- *At least one*: "at least one of $E_1, E_2$ is zero" is the
  multiplication-table reading of a product being zero,
  $E_1 \cdot E_2 = \texttt{0}$.
- *Both*: when $E_1$ and $E_2$ are themselves yes/no values, "both
  are zero" is the same as "neither is one," which by the dictionary
  is $(\texttt{1} + E_1)(\texttt{1} + E_2) = \texttt{1}$.

In practice it is usually easier to keep the two equations as a
pair and reason with both at once.

## Worked translations

**"All three of $a, b, c$ are yes."** A chain of "both"s, so
$abc = \texttt{1}$.

**"At least one of $a, b, c$ is no."** Negation of the previous —
$abc = \texttt{0}$.

**"$a$ and $b$ agree, and $b$ and $c$ agree."** $a + b = \texttt{0}$
and $b + c = \texttt{0}$. Adding the two equations gives
$a + c = \texttt{0}$ — agreement is contagious along a chain.

**"If $a$ and $b$ are both yes, then $c$ is no."** Using "if yes,
then yes" on the condensed condition: failure pattern is
$ab \cdot (\texttt{1} + (\texttt{1} + c)) = abc$, so the claim
itself is $abc = \texttt{0}$.

**"Exactly one of $a, b, c$ is yes."** Three mutually-exclusive
ways, sum the truth values:

$$
a(\texttt{1} + b)(\texttt{1} + c) + b(\texttt{1} + a)(\texttt{1} + c) + c(\texttt{1} + a)(\texttt{1} + b) = \texttt{1}.
$$

Expanding, the three-term sum collapses to
$a + b + c + abc = \texttt{1}$.

**"Exactly two of $a, b, c$ are yes."** Pick which two:

$$
ab(\texttt{1} + c) + ac(\texttt{1} + b) + bc(\texttt{1} + a) = \texttt{1},
$$

which expands to $ab + ac + bc + abc = \texttt{1}$. Compare with
the previous: the three-variable boolean world has three clean
polynomials — $abc$, $a + b + c + abc$, and $ab + ac + bc + abc$
— that cut cleanly through the eight yes/no patterns and single
out "all three," "exactly one," and "exactly two" respectively.
