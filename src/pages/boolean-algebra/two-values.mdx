The algebra of this chapter — a tiny arithmetic in which the only
numbers are $\texttt{0}$ and $\texttt{1}$ — is the work, almost
single-handedly, of the English schoolmaster *George Boole*. In
$1847$ he published a slim pamphlet, *The Mathematical Analysis of
Logic*, with the audacious thesis that the laws of human reasoning
could be cast as the laws of an algebra. What Boole noticed was
simple but startling: if one writes $\texttt{1}$ for "true" and
$\texttt{0}$ for "false," then *and* behaves like multiplication and
*or* (in its exclusive form) behaves like addition — with the single
peculiarity that $\texttt{1} + \texttt{1}$, having no logical room
to be $2$, must collapse back to $\texttt{0}$.

## The two tables

The whole of this chapter takes place inside a tiny world: a world
that contains only two numbers, $\texttt{0}$ and $\texttt{1}$. We
define addition and multiplication on this little world and watch
the algebra spring out of those two definitions. To keep the two
worlds visually distinct, we write boolean values in a typewriter
font — $\texttt{0}$ and $\texttt{1}$ — rather than the ordinary $0$
and $1$.

Three of the four rows in each table look exactly like the answers
ordinary integer arithmetic would give. The single row that breaks
ranks is the bottom row of the addition table:

$$
\texttt{1} + \texttt{1} = \texttt{0},
$$

not $2$. There is a good reason for this rule.

## A coarse view of ordinary arithmetic

The strange-looking rule $\texttt{1} + \texttt{1} = \texttt{0}$
becomes natural the moment we read $\texttt{0}$ as "even" and
$\texttt{1}$ as "odd."

<div style={{textAlign:'center', margin:'12px 0'}}>
$\texttt{0} = $ even, $\quad \texttt{1} = $ odd.
</div>

Now look back at the addition table.

- *Even plus even is even*: $\texttt{0} + \texttt{0} = \texttt{0}$.
- *Even plus odd is odd*: $\texttt{0} + \texttt{1} = \texttt{1} + \texttt{0} = \texttt{1}$.
- *Odd plus odd is even*: $\texttt{1} + \texttt{1} = \texttt{0}$.

The boolean addition table is just the parity arithmetic of ordinary
integers — it records what happens to the even-or-odd-ness of a
sum, and forgets every other detail.

The same coarse reading works for multiplication: even times
anything is even ($\texttt{0} \cdot a = \texttt{0}$), and odd times
odd is odd ($\texttt{1} \cdot \texttt{1} = \texttt{1}$).

So boolean arithmetic is not a foreign new system. It is the
familiar arithmetic of integers, viewed at a deliberately coarsened
resolution: every number is reported only by its even/odd label,
and everything else is thrown away.

## A second reading: yes and no

The same two values, $\texttt{0}$ and $\texttt{1}$, will play
another role in this chapter. We will use $\texttt{1}$ to mean
"yes / true" and $\texttt{0}$ to mean "no / false," so we can talk
about logical conditions in the same algebra.

If this second reading gives you a moment's pause, you are paying
attention. Under the parity reading, the equation
$\texttt{1} + \texttt{1} = \texttt{0}$ translates to *odd plus odd
is even*, a familiar fact. Under the yes/no reading, the very same
equation translates to *yes plus yes is no*, which does not seem
to mean anything at all.

Nothing has gone wrong. This is precisely the freedom that
abstraction buys us. Boolean arithmetic is a skeleton: the
"odd / even" reading is one coat we can put on it, the "yes / no"
reading is another. Meaning is attached for our convenience, and
peeled off again the moment it stops helping.
