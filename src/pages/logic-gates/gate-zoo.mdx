A computer is a great wall of little boxes. Each box has some wires
going in on the left and one wire coming out on the right. Each
wire is in one of two states — *carrying current* (we'll call it
$\texttt{1}$) or *not carrying current* ($\texttt{0}$) — and the
rule connecting the inputs to the output is fixed in advance and
listens only to the *current* values on the wires. The boxes are
called **logic gates**, and a wiring of them together is called a
**circuit**.

Every diagram on this page is live. Click an input knob to flip it
between $\texttt{0}$ and $\texttt{1}$, and watch the output respond.
The truth table beside each circuit highlights the row matching the
current inputs.

## NOT — the flipper

The simplest gate has one input and one output, and its job is to
flip whatever comes in. Its shape — a triangle pointing right with
a tiny circle stuck to its tip — will be a standing companion. The
circle is the universal "and then flip" mark.

<CircuitDiagram client:visible circuit={notGate} showTruthTable />

We write the output of NOT on input $a$ as $\mathord{\texttt{!}}a$.
On the algebraic side it is the simplest possible expression:

$$
\mathord{\texttt{!}}a = a + \texttt{1}.
$$

(Check: $\texttt{0} + \texttt{1} = \texttt{1}$ and
$\texttt{1} + \texttt{1} = \texttt{0}$.) From now on, whenever a
circuit feeds a wire $a$ through a NOT gate, we may equally well
write the output as $a + \texttt{1}$ in our algebra and forget that
there ever was a gate.

## AND — both must say yes

The AND gate has two inputs. It outputs $\texttt{1}$ exactly when
*both* inputs are $\texttt{1}$. A useful mental picture: AND is a
strict committee of two. Its shape is a flat-backed "D" — a plain
back with a rounded nose.

<CircuitDiagram client:visible circuit={andGate} showTruthTable />

We write the output as $a \mathbin{\texttt{\&}} b$. Lined up against
boolean multiplication, the columns agree row for row, so

$$
a \mathbin{\texttt{\&}} b = ab.
$$

The strict committee *is* boolean multiplication.

## OR — at least one

The OR gate outputs $\texttt{1}$ whenever *at least one* of its
inputs is $\texttt{1}$. It outputs $\texttt{0}$ only when both are
$\texttt{0}$. OR is a generous committee: it cheers if anyone
cheers. Its shape is a shield with a curved, swept-back back.

<CircuitDiagram client:visible circuit={orGate} showTruthTable />

We write the output as $a \mathbin{\texttt{|}\hspace{-0.1em}\texttt{|}} b$.
Lined up against $a + b + ab$:

$$
a \mathbin{\texttt{|}\hspace{-0.1em}\texttt{|}} b = a + b + ab.
$$

A reading note. The bottom row evaluates to
$\texttt{1} + \texttt{1} + \texttt{1}\cdot\texttt{1} = \texttt{0} + \texttt{1} = \texttt{1}$,
which is what we want. The surprise that boolean
$\texttt{1} + \texttt{1} = \texttt{0}$ would otherwise produce on its
own is repaired by the extra $ab$ term.

A small trap worth naming: the everyday English word "or" is often
used to mean *one or the other, but not both* ("would you like tea
or coffee?"). The OR gate is **not** that gate. OR is happy with
both. The picky one is below.

## XOR — the disagreement detector

XOR (pronounced "ex-or," short for *exclusive or*) outputs
$\texttt{1}$ exactly when its two inputs *disagree*. We write the
output as $a \oplus b$.

<CircuitDiagram client:visible circuit={xorGate} showTruthTable />

Side by side with the boolean sum $a + b$, the columns agree, so

$$
a \oplus b = a + b.
$$

The disagreement detector is, on the algebraic side, nothing but
boolean addition. Its shape is the OR shape with a second curve
peeled back behind it — as if announcing "I am OR, but pickier."

## A gate that always says no

A small curiosity that uses no new vocabulary. Can we build a
one-input gadget whose output is $\texttt{0}$ *no matter* what the
input is? At first this sounds either silly or impossible — AND, OR,
and NOT all *listen* to their inputs. The trick is to make two
listening parts *contradict* each other. Take $a$, feed one copy
straight in, and feed another copy through a NOT first:

<CircuitDiagram client:visible circuit={alwaysZero} steppable showTruthTable />

Press **Step ▸** to walk through the gates one at a time. Whatever
you set $a$ to, the two inputs of the AND can never agree on the
value $\texttt{1}$, so the AND's strict-committee rule is never
satisfied. The output is $\texttt{0}$ on every input. We have built
a deaf "always-no" gadget out of two listening parts.

The algebra says the same thing in one line:

$$
a \cdot (a + \texttt{1}) = a^2 + a = a + a = \texttt{0},
$$

using $a^2 = a$ and $a + a = \texttt{0}$.

## Three inputs, no new gate

The fire-alarm wish — a box that lights up the moment *any one* of
several detectors sees smoke — has three or more inputs. A natural
worry: do we need a brand-new "three-input OR" gate? No. The
two-input OR we already have is enough. Use it twice:

<CircuitDiagram client:visible circuit={orChain3} steppable showTruthTable />

Read along the wires. The first OR reports $\texttt{1}$ exactly
when at least one of $a$ or $b$ is $\texttt{1}$. The second OR
takes that report and $c$. Putting them together: the final wire
carries $\texttt{1}$ exactly when at least one of $a, b, c$ is
$\texttt{1}$. The same idea keeps working past three: $n$ inputs
can be ORed together using $n - 1$ ordinary two-input ORs.
