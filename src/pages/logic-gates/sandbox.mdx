Imagine a small wooden box with rods sticking out of either side. You
push a rod on the left — something clicks inside — and a rod on the
right either pops out or pulls back in. We say the rods on the left
are *inputs* and the ones on the right are *outputs*, and we read the
state of each rod as either **0** (pulled out for an input, retracted
for an output) or **1** (pushed in for an input, sticking out for an
output).

Our question: can we arrange enough rods, pivots, and rigid bars
inside the box so that the output rod does exactly what we want —
for *every* combination of inputs? If we can, we have built a
mechanical **logic gate**.

Below is a workbench. The pieces you have are

- **sleeves**, which hold a rod in a single line and let it slide,
- **inputs** (click the red knob to push the rod in; click again to
  pull it out),
- **outputs**, whose state is read off the dashed knob,
- **revolute joints**, pin-points about which rigid pieces can turn
  freely,
- **connectors**, which are rigid bars between two revolute joints,
  and
- **springs**, decorative pieces that remind you what is pushing back.

With these six primitives you can build any mechanical gate you like.
A revolute joint placed in *empty space* is anchored to the workbench
(we draw a little ground-tick beneath it); a joint placed on *a rod or
a connector* rides with that piece. Two or more pieces pinned at the
same joint can swing freely relative to one another — that is the
entire mechanical content of a pivot.

**Tools.**
*Select* clicks bodies (shift-click adds to the selection).
*Drag* moves a body (or a whole group) around the grid.
*Revolute* places a joint; clicking an existing joint merges with it,
letting you pin, say, a connector to a rod at the connector's
midpoint. *Connector* wants two joint clicks and will draw a rigid
bar between them. *Anchor* toggles a frame-anchor tick on any joint
you click.

**Setting the initial state of inputs and outputs.** A gate design
normally begins with a statement like *"when the input is 0, the
output should be 1"*. You can set up exactly that on the workbench
before wiring anything: click an input's knob to put it in state 1
(or leave it at state 0), and click an output's dashed readout to
flip it between 1 and 0. Once the output is wired into the mechanism
the solver will take over, but this lets you arrange the pieces so
the *rest configuration* is the relationship you want.

**The Bisector tool.** Many gate designs demand that some piece sit
*equidistant* from two other points — for instance, a shared pivot
that lies on the line equidistant from the input's end and the
output's end. The Bisector tool gives you that line as a dashed
guide: click two joints (or two empty points) and the perpendicular
bisector appears, following the joints if you drag them later.

**Your first exercise — build an inverter.**
Place the input horizontally at, say, (5, 6) and leave it at state 0.
Place the output horizontally at (13, 6) and click its readout to set
it to state 1. Put a revolute joint on the input's far end and
another on the output's near end; these will sit at two points that
you now want to connect to a shared, equidistant pivot. Click
**Bisector**, then click those two joints — a vertical guide appears
at x = 9.

Place a *sleeve* somewhere on that guide line (say at (9, 3), oriented
vertically). Place a revolute joint at one end of the sleeve's rod,
on the bisector. Now use the Connector tool twice: once from the
input-end joint to the new joint on the sleeve rod, once from the
output-end joint to it. The two connectors are equal-length (because
of the bisector), and the sleeve rod can only slide along the guide
line, so when you push the input in the sleeve rod slides and the
output retracts by the same amount. You have built a **NOT** gate —
a mechanism that flips its input.

**Tips.** Press **R** while a placement tool is active to flip
orientation. **Esc** returns to Select. **Delete** removes a
selection. When you are happy with a mechanism, name it and press
**Save** — and when you want a clean view for class, select the
internals, press **Group**, then **Scaffold** to hide them behind a
haze.
