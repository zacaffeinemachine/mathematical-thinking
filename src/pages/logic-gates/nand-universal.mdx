So far we have allowed three kinds of gates: AND, OR, and NOT. The
recipe of the previous page shows that this toolkit is *enough* —
enough to realize any table whatsoever. A natural next question:
is it *minimum*? Can we do it with fewer kinds of gates?

Remarkably, the answer is yes, and in a surprisingly extreme way.
There is a *single* two-input gate from which all of AND, OR, and
NOT can be rebuilt. Once you have that one gate on your shelf, you
never need to buy any of the other three again.

## Meet NAND

The gate is called **NAND**, and it is simply AND followed by NOT.
NAND outputs $\texttt{1}$ unless *both* of its inputs are
$\texttt{1}$, in which case it outputs $\texttt{0}$. Its shape is
the familiar AND "D" with a little circle pinned to the nose — the
same "and then flip" circle you saw on NOT.

<CircuitDiagram client:visible circuit={nandGate} showTruthTable />

Its table is the AND table with the output column flipped.

## NAND builds NOT

To build a NOT from a NAND, feed the same wire $a$ into *both* of
NAND's inputs.

<CircuitDiagram client:visible circuit={nandNot} steppable showTruthTable />

Check the table: if $a = \texttt{0}$, the inputs are
$(\texttt{0}, \texttt{0})$ and NAND outputs $\texttt{1}$; if
$a = \texttt{1}$, the inputs are $(\texttt{1}, \texttt{1})$ and
NAND outputs $\texttt{0}$. Input $a$, output the flip of $a$. That
is a NOT.

## NAND builds AND

NAND is AND followed by NOT. So to recover AND, we just have to
undo the NOT — that is, chain another NOT on top of NAND. But we
just showed how to build a NOT out of NANDs by feeding the same
wire into both inputs:

<CircuitDiagram client:visible circuit={nandAnd} steppable showTruthTable />

Two NANDs, a little tangle of wires, and you have recovered AND.

## NAND builds OR

This one takes a moment of thought. Recall that
$a \mathbin{\texttt{|}\hspace{-0.1em}\texttt{|}} b$ is $\texttt{1}$
unless both $a$ and $b$ are $\texttt{0}$, and notice that the
second half of that sentence is exactly *not (both $a$ and $b$ are
$\texttt{0}$)*. If we first flip $a$ and $b$ individually, and
*then* feed those into a NAND, we get this:

<CircuitDiagram client:visible circuit={nandOr} steppable showTruthTable />

The only way for the two inputs of the final NAND to both be
$\texttt{1}$ is for $a$ and $b$ both to be $\texttt{0}$; in that
case the NAND outputs $\texttt{0}$. In every other case at least
one of the flipped inputs is $\texttt{0}$, the NAND sees a
$\texttt{0}$ and outputs $\texttt{1}$. That is the OR table. And
since each flipping NOT is itself a NAND with its inputs tied
together, the whole circuit is made entirely out of NANDs.

## NAND is enough on its own

Putting the last three constructions together:

<div style={{
  margin:'20px 0',
  padding:'14px 18px',
  borderLeft:'4px solid var(--accent)',
  background:'var(--surface)',
  borderRadius:4,
}}>

Every circuit that can be built out of AND, OR, and NOT gates can
also be built using only NAND gates.

</div>

And we already know, from the previous page, that AND, OR, and
NOT are enough to realize every possible table. Combining the two
statements: NAND, on its own, is enough to realize every possible
table. **A single kind of gate, replicated and wired up in enough
copies, can compute anything a circuit can compute.**

This is not a party trick. Real digital chips are designed around
exactly this principle: manufacture one kind of gate very, very
well, and then build the entire machine out of billions of copies
of it.
