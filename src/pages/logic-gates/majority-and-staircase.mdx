Two small case studies of the recipe in action — one a vote, the
other a hallway light. Both take three inputs.

## A majority signal

Three friends decide something by majority vote. Each presses a
button: $\texttt{1}$ for *yes*, $\texttt{0}$ for *no*. A single
lamp on the wall should light up exactly when at least two of the
three have said yes.

The recipe-on-autopilot circuit has eight rows in its table; the
winning rows are
$(\texttt{1},\texttt{1},\texttt{0}),\;(\texttt{1},\texttt{0},\texttt{1}),\;(\texttt{0},\texttt{1},\texttt{1}),\;(\texttt{1},\texttt{1},\texttt{1})$.
The straight recipe would build four spike detectors and OR them.
Clunky-but-correct.

A nicer circuit reads the rule directly: *at least two of $a, b, c$
are $\texttt{1}$* is the same as *at least one of the pairs
$(a, b), (a, c), (b, c)$ consists entirely of $\texttt{1}$s*. Three
ANDs, two ORs:

<CircuitDiagram client:visible circuit={majority3} steppable showTruthTable />

Each of the four winning rows has at least one all-$\texttt{1}$s
pair, so at least one AND fires; each of the four losing rows has
no all-$\texttt{1}$s pair, so no AND fires. Two circuits, same
table. Both are correct. The second is shorter. This is your first
glimpse that the recipe does not have the last word: the same
behaviour can often be built in many ways, and some of them are
considerably smaller than others.

## The staircase, two switches

Recall the staircase: a switch at the top, a switch at the bottom,
a single bulb, and the wish that flipping either switch on its own
toggles the bulb. Filling in the two-input table by experiment
gives a column whose only $\texttt{1}$s sit on the rows where
exactly one of the two switches is up — the XOR table:

<CircuitDiagram client:visible circuit={staircase2} showTruthTable />

The two-switch staircase, in other words, is wired as $a \oplus b$.

## The staircase, three switches

Now imagine the staircase is so long that a third switch has been
installed at the landing halfway up. The wish is the same as
before, just with one more switch in the picture: *each switch,
flipped on its own, must flip the state of the bulb, no matter
what the other two are doing*.

Fill in the eight-row table by the same kind of experiment. Start
with all three switches at $\texttt{0}$ and the bulb off. Flipping
exactly one switch must change the bulb — so every row that
differs from $(\texttt{0}, \texttt{0}, \texttt{0})$ in exactly one
position must have the bulb *on*. Flipping two switches must leave
the bulb back where it started. Flipping all three switches must,
once again, put the bulb in the *on* state. The bulb is on
exactly when an *odd* number of the three switches are at
$\texttt{1}$. That is a chain of XORs:

<CircuitDiagram client:visible circuit={xorChain3} steppable showTruthTable />

And this pattern does not stop at three. If you install *any*
number of switches along the staircase and insist that each one,
on its own, flip the bulb, then the only wiring that grants the
wish is: the bulb is on exactly when an odd number of switches are
at $\texttt{1}$. That is an XOR chain of arbitrary length — each
new switch adds one more XOR at the end.
