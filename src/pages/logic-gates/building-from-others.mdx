The four gates from the previous page might at first look
independent. They are not. We will see that AND can already be
built out of NOT and OR; that XOR can be built out of all three;
and that NOT itself, on the other hand, is essential — no
combination of ANDs and ORs can flip a wire.

## AND from NOT and OR

The recipe is short: *flip the inputs, OR them, flip the result*.
Click **Step ▸** below to walk the signal through the four gates,
or just toggle $a$ and $b$ and watch the final wire.

<CircuitDiagram client:visible circuit={andFromNotOr} steppable showTruthTable />

Why does this circuit compute $a \mathbin{\texttt{\&}} b$? Translate
each gate into its algebraic dictionary and follow the wire from
left to right. After the first two NOTs the inner wires carry
$a + \texttt{1}$ and $b + \texttt{1}$. The OR combines them as

$$
(a + \texttt{1}) + (b + \texttt{1}) + (a + \texttt{1})(b + \texttt{1}).
$$

Expand the product, $(a + \texttt{1})(b + \texttt{1}) = ab + a + b + \texttt{1}$,
and collect, using $a + a = \texttt{0}$ and
$\texttt{1} + \texttt{1} = \texttt{0}$ to kill doubled terms:

$$
\;= (a + a) + (b + b) + (\texttt{1} + \texttt{1} + \texttt{1}) + ab = \texttt{1} + ab.
$$

The outermost NOT adds $\texttt{1}$:
$\mathord{\texttt{!}}(\texttt{1} + ab) = ab$. The output is exactly
$ab = a \mathbin{\texttt{\&}} b$, even though no AND gate appears
anywhere inside.

The pattern — *flip the inputs, OR them, flip the result* — is one
half of a famous pair of identities called **De Morgan's laws**.
The other half builds OR from NOT and AND in the mirror-image way.

## XOR from AND, OR, and NOT

We called XOR a new gate, but in fact we do not need a new box on
the shelf. The "disagree" signal can be assembled out of the three
gates we already have.

Two inputs $a$ and $b$ disagree exactly when one is $\texttt{1}$
while the other is $\texttt{0}$. So either ($a = \texttt{1}$ and
$b = \texttt{0}$) or ($a = \texttt{0}$ and $b = \texttt{1}$). Each
of these is a small AND; gluing them with an OR gives:

<CircuitDiagram client:visible circuit={xorFromBasics} steppable showTruthTable />

Each upper branch of the OR catches one of the two "disagree" rows
of the table, and the outer OR glues the two catches together. This
is the first hint of a much bigger fact: any gate we might want to
invent can be built from just AND, OR, and NOT. The next page
makes this precise.

## Can NOT be built from AND and OR alone?

We have just seen that the strict committee AND can be assembled
out of NOT and OR. The natural follow-up is: can the trade go the
other way? Can the flipper NOT be assembled out of AND and OR
alone?

The answer is **no**, and the reason is that AND and OR are both
*listeners-only-upward*: feeding them all-$\texttt{0}$s produces a
$\texttt{0}$, and feeding them all-$\texttt{1}$s produces a
$\texttt{1}$. Whatever circuit we cook up out of AND and OR
inherits this politeness — on input $a = \texttt{0}$, every wire
inside is $\texttt{0}$, so the output is $\texttt{0}$; on input
$a = \texttt{1}$, every wire inside is $\texttt{1}$, so the output
is $\texttt{1}$. But NOT does the opposite.

On the algebraic side, the same fact is even shorter to read off.
AND is $ab$ and OR is $a + b + ab$; both expressions evaluate to
$\texttt{0}$ when all inputs are $\texttt{0}$, and any combination
inherits this. But $\mathord{\texttt{!}}a = a + \texttt{1}$
evaluates to $\texttt{1}$ at $a = \texttt{0}$. The $\texttt{1}$ has
to come in from *somewhere*, and AND and OR alone do not supply it.

Moral: the trio NOT, AND, OR is not a free-for-all. AND is
removable. NOT, on the other hand, is *essential*: any further
trimming of the alphabet must hold on to a gate that flips.
