For most of recorded history, infinity was a word mathematicians used
uneasily and tried not to use at all. Aristotle had ruled, in the
fourth century BCE, that one could speak of a *potential* infinity —
a process that could be continued for as long as one liked — but
never of an *actual* infinity given all at once. Two thousand years
later, this verdict was still in force. *Galileo Galilei*, in the
*Two New Sciences* of $1638$, noticed that the perfect squares can
be paired one-for-one with the positive integers
($1 \leftrightarrow 1$, $2 \leftrightarrow 4$, $3 \leftrightarrow 9$, …),
even though most positive integers are not squares; rather than draw
a conclusion, he concluded sorrowfully that the words "equal,"
"greater," and "less" simply could not be applied to infinite
collections at all.

The man who finally faced it was the German mathematician *Georg
Cantor*, working in the otherwise quiet university town of Halle in
the $1870$s and $1880$s. In a paper of $1874$ Cantor proved, for the
first time, that there are strictly more real numbers than integers
— that the apparent unity of the word "infinite" conceals a
hierarchy of distinct sizes. Seventeen years later he gave the
cleaner proof we use today, the diagonal argument that closes this
chapter, and showed in passing that the same trick works for almost
any collection one cares to consider. *David Hilbert*, in $1926$,
declared that "no one shall expel us from the paradise that Cantor
has created" — and it was the same Hilbert, in a popular lecture,
who first told the story of the hotel with infinitely many rooms.

What survives, stripped of the polemic, is a small handful of
arguments of extraordinary clarity. The diagonal argument is one of
them. The dovetailing argument that lists pairs of natural numbers
along finite diagonals is another. The bijection trick that lets a
fully booked hotel accommodate one more guest is a third. None of
these requires more than the patience to read a single page of
bookkeeping; together they show that infinity is not one thing but
a country with at least two distinct cities, and that the boundary
between them is sharp.

Our exploration will not begin with dry formulas, but with puzzles.
Let's play a game.

## A simple guessing game

I am thinking of a positive whole number. You may ask me any
sequence of yes/no questions you like, and I promise to answer each
one truthfully. Your task is to identify my number.

The catch: you must commit, in advance, to a strategy that is
*guaranteed* to pin down my number in finitely many questions, no
matter which positive whole number I picked.

Can you do it?

A first reaction might be: "of course — ask 'is it less than $100$?',
then narrow it down." But what if my number is $10^{100}$? Or
$10^{10^{100}}$? You have no upper bound on what I might be
thinking of. The set of possibilities is infinite.

And yet there is a strategy that works. Ask, in order:

<div style={{textAlign:'center', margin:'12px 0'}}>
"Is it $1$?" &nbsp; "Is it $2$?" &nbsp; "Is it $3$?" &nbsp; "Is it $4$?" &nbsp; …
</div>

Whatever number $N$ I picked, my answer to your $N$-th question
will be "yes," and the game ends. Every positive whole number is
reached in finite time, simply because every positive whole number
appears at some finite position in the list $1, 2, 3, \ldots$.

The reason this works is so plain it is easy to miss: the
possibilities can be arranged in a single list, and your strategy
walks down the list one step at a time. Hold on to this idea — it
is the engine of the entire chapter.

## Raising the stakes

Now suppose I tell you my number could be *any* integer — positive,
negative, or zero. Can you still guarantee to find it in finite
time?

The naive list $0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots$ now misses every negative
number. But here is a fix: interleave the positives and negatives.
Ask in the order

$$
0,\ 1,\ -1,\ 2,\ -2,\ 3,\ -3,\ \ldots
$$

Every integer — positive, negative, or zero — appears at some
finite position in this new list, so the strategy still terminates.
The trick is to weave two infinite lists together into one.

Push further: suppose my number is a fraction $p/q$ (with $p$ a
whole number and $q$ a positive whole number). The fractions look
much wilder than the integers — between any two of them lies
another. Can you still pin one down with yes/no questions?

The remarkable answer is yes, and the technique — weaving a
two-dimensional grid of possibilities into a single list — is
exactly what we will need to disarm an upgraded version of the
puzzle in the next sections. We will return to the fractions once
we have that technique in hand.

The chapter is, in essence, the story of which infinite collections
*can* be threaded into a single list and which cannot. Let us begin
with a puzzle that demands exactly this kind of threading.
