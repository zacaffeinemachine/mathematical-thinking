## A harder version of the guessing game

Recall the guessing game from the start of the chapter. I was
thinking of a positive whole number, and you found a yes/no
questioning strategy — "is it $1$?, is it $2$?, is it $3$?, …" —
that was guaranteed to identify my number in finitely many
questions. The same trick, with a little weaving, handled all
integers, and (with the diagonal sweep of the previous sections)
it would handle all fractions too.

Let us now play a much harder version.

I am no longer thinking of a number. I am thinking of an *infinite*
sequence of yeses and nos — one answer for each of the positions
$1, 2, 3, \ldots$. So my secret might begin

<div style={{textAlign:'center', margin:'12px 0'}}>
yes, no, no, yes, yes, no, yes, no, no, no, …
</div>

and continue forever in some pattern only I know.

You may ask any yes/no questions you like, in any order, and I
will answer each one truthfully. Your goal is to commit, in
advance, to a strategy that is *guaranteed* to pin down my entire
sequence in finitely many questions, no matter which sequence I
picked.

Can you do it?

## Why no strategy can win

The answer is **no**, and the reason is exactly the reason the
unwritable book could not be written.

Any strategy you commit to is, in the end, a recipe. Run the
recipe against my sequence and it asks some first question, then a
second, and so on; after finitely many of these it announces a
guess. The crucial point is that there are only countably many
possible scripts the strategy can follow during a play of the
game — one for each finite transcript of yeses and nos — and so
over all possible plays, the strategy can produce *at most* a
countable list of guessed sequences.

Call those guessed sequences $G_1, G_2, G_3, \ldots$. If your
strategy works, then for every secret sequence $S$ I might choose,
there is some play of the game where the strategy guesses $S$
correctly — so $S$ must appear somewhere on the list
$G_1, G_2, G_3, \ldots$. In other words, your strategy gives a
*single list of all possible infinite yes/no sequences*.

But this is exactly the unwritable book in a new costume. Identify
"yes" with "include this page" and "no" with "leave this page out."
Then an infinite sequence of yeses and nos is the same thing as a
reading list, and your hypothetical list $G_1, G_2, G_3, \ldots$
is the same thing as a complete catalogue of reading lists — which
we have just proved cannot exist.

Concretely, given $G_1, G_2, G_3, \ldots$, build a new sequence
$D$ as follows: in position $n$, put the *opposite* of whatever
$G_n$ has in position $n$. Then $D$ disagrees with $G_n$ at
position $n$, so $D$ is not $G_n$ — and this is true for every
$n$. The sequence $D$ is on no page of your strategy's catalogue.
If I happen to have chosen $D$ as my secret, your strategy is
doomed.

## The same diamond, two settings

Notice the parallel between the two impossibility results.

- In the unwritable book, we tried to list every reading list as a
  page of a book. Cantor's diagonal produced a reading list $R$
  that disagreed with the page-$n$ list at the question "is $n$ in
  the list?"
- In the guessing game, we tried to list every infinite yes/no
  sequence as a possible outcome of a strategy. The same diagonal
  produced a sequence $D$ that disagreed with the $n$-th outcome
  at position $n$.

The two problems look completely different on the surface — one is
about writing books, the other about asking questions — but
underneath they are the same problem, and Cantor's argument
dispatches both with a single move.

A reading list, viewed correctly, is just an infinite sequence of
yeses and nos: for each page, "yes I include it" or "no I do not."
The collection of all reading lists and the collection of all
infinite yes/no sequences are not just the same size; they are
literally the same collection in different clothes. And we have
now shown, twice, that this collection cannot be threaded into a
single list.

This is the chapter's final lesson. The natural numbers, the
integers, the fractions, the pairs, the triples — all the
infinities we met can be tamed by a clever listing. But the
infinity of yes/no sequences — equivalently, the infinity of
reading lists, and (as a course in real analysis later reveals)
the infinity of points on a line — is of a strictly larger kind.
No strategy, no book, no enumeration whatsoever can reach all of
them.

Cantor's infinite book has pages numbered $1, 2, 3, \ldots$.

- A **reading list** is a set of page numbers (finite or infinite).
- A **finite reading list** is a set of finitely many pages.
- An **even reading list** is a set of even page numbers.
- A **finite even reading list** is a set of finitely many even page numbers.

Choose the correct options.

**(a)** One can write a reading list on each page in such a way
that the book then features all possible reading lists.

**(b)** One can write an even reading list on each page in such a
way that the book then features all possible even reading lists.

**(c)** One can write a finite reading list on each page in such
a way that the book then features all possible finite reading
lists.

**(d)** One can write a finite even reading list on each page in
such a way that the book then features all possible finite even
reading lists.
