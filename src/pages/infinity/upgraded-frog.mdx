## Upgraded frog

Let us raise the stakes. The frog is now more sophisticated: at the
buzzer it secretly picks *two* natural numbers, a starting offset
$d$ and a jump size $k$. At time $t$ its position is

$$
d + tk.
$$

As before, the shooter with a water gun wants to guarantee a
splash, and the entire plan must be written down before the frog
makes its choice. The question becomes: *can the shooter devise a
single plan that guarantees a hit, no matter which pair $(d, k)$
the frog chooses?*

## Taming the two-dimensional infinity

The set of choices available to the frog is now the set of all
pairs of natural numbers, which we denote by $\mathbb{N} \times \mathbb{N}$.
For example, if the frog picks $(d, k) = (5, 2)$, it traces out the
pads $7, 9, 11, 13, \ldots$ at times $t = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$.

At first sight we seem to have made the problem infinitely harder.
Instead of a single infinite set of choices, the frog now has an
infinite family of infinite choices. Can the pairs of natural
numbers even be arranged into a single list?

Remarkably, they can. The set $\mathbb{N} \times \mathbb{N}$ is
also listable. The trick is not to march straight across or
straight down — either sweep would leave entire rows or columns
untouched forever — but to sweep along the *diagonals*, where each
diagonal contains only finitely many pairs and is therefore
exhausted in finitely many steps before we move on to the next.
This method is often called **dovetailing**, and it is due to
Cantor.

The diagonal enumeration of $\mathbb{N} \times \mathbb{N}$. Position

The resulting list begins

$$
(1,1), \quad (2,1), \quad (1,2), \quad (3,1), \quad (2,2), \quad (1,3), \quad \ldots,
$$

and every pair $(d, k)$ eventually appears somewhere in it. Let us
write $(d_n, k_n)$ for the $n$-th pair in the list.

The shooter's strategy now writes itself. At time $n$, target the
frog corresponding to the $n$-th pair in the list:

<div style={{textAlign:'center', fontStyle:'italic', margin:'12px 0'}}>
at time $n$, shoot at pad $d_n + n k_n$.
</div>

Suppose the frog chose the pair $(d, k)$. This pair sits somewhere
in our list — say at position $m$, so $(d, k) = (d_m, k_m)$.

- *shooter's action at time $m$*: shoots at pad $d_m + m k_m$;
- *frog's position at time $m$*: pad $d + m k = d_m + m k_m$.

Another direct hit.

The victory rests entirely on the fact that *every* pair of natural
numbers could be lined up in a single list. This hints at a deep
fact: the "amount" of infinity in $\mathbb{N} \times \mathbb{N}$ is
no greater than the "amount" of infinity in $\mathbb{N}$. Both sets
are countably infinite.

## The explicit strategy

The diagonal enumeration above can be written down as an explicit
formula. To do so, we first identify which diagonal the $n$-th
pair $(d_n, k_n)$ lives on. In our listing, the pairs with
$d + k = s$ form the $s$-th diagonal, and this diagonal contains
$s - 1$ pairs. The diagonals strictly before the $s$-th one
therefore account for a total of

$$
1 + 2 + \cdots + (s - 2) = \frac{(s - 2)(s - 1)}{2}
$$

pairs. Solving for the smallest $s$ whose cumulative count reaches
$n$ gives the diagonal index

$$
s_n = \left\lfloor \frac{\sqrt{8n - 7} + 3}{2} \right\rfloor.
$$

Once $s_n$ is known, the position of $n$ within its diagonal pins
down the two coordinates:

$$
d_n = \frac{s_n(s_n - 1)}{2} - n + 1,
\qquad
k_n = n - \frac{(s_n - 2)(s_n - 1)}{2}.
$$

Our strategy is therefore to shoot, at time $n$, the pad

$$
f(n) = d_n + n\, k_n
= \left(\frac{s_n(s_n - 1)}{2} - n + 1\right) + n\!\left(n - \frac{(s_n - 2)(s_n - 1)}{2}\right).
$$

Running the correspondence in the other direction, the pair $(d, k)$
appears at position

$$
g(d, k) = \frac{(d + k - 2)(d + k - 1)}{2} + k
$$

in the list, so the frog $(d, k)$ gets soaked the moment the clock
hits $t = g(d, k)$.

Picture an infinite, icy-cold pond. Stretching out in both
directions are lilypads, conveniently numbered by all the
integers:

$$
\ldots, -3, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots
$$

A mischievous frog, code-named *Hopper*, sits on pad $0$. At time
$0$, a buzzer sounds, and Hopper secretly chooses two **integers**
$d$ and $k$ (an integer can be positive, negative, or $0$). His
position at any future time $t$ is $d + tk$. Note that he could be
jumping forwards (if $k > 0$), backwards (if $k < 0$), or sitting
on pad $d$ (if $k = 0$).

The shooter must write down the entire plan in advance — a list of
shots of the form (pad number, time). Can the shooter devise one
single, universal plan that guarantees Hopper gets wet, no matter
what secret integers $d$ and $k$ Hopper chooses?

A frog named *Ribbit* sits on the centre of a 2-dimensional grid
of lilypads, indexed by two integers, currently at pad $(0, 0)$.
At time $0$ a buzzer sounds, and Ribbit secretly chooses two
**integers** $a$ and $b$. His position at time $t$ is $(at, bt^2)$
for $t = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$.

For example, if Ribbit chose $a = 2$ and $b = 3$:

- at time $1$ he is at $(2, 3)$;
- at time $2$ he is at $(4, 12)$;
- at time $3$ he is at $(6, 27)$;
- $\vdots$

The shooter must write down a single pre-determined shooting plan:
at time $t$, shoot at some pad address (a function of $t$). The
shooter wants to guarantee *two* splashes on Ribbit.

Can the shooter devise one universal plan that guarantees Ribbit
gets soaked twice, regardless of which integers $a$ and $b$ he
chooses? If yes, describe the plan and show it succeeds. If no,
prove that this cannot be done.
</div>
