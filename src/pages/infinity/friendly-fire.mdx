## The puzzle

Imagine a frog sitting on a very long line of lily pads, numbered
$0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots$. The frog starts at lily pad $0$. When a
buzzer sounds, the frog secretly chooses a natural number $k$ (so
$k$ could be $1$, $2$, $3$, or any other natural number). Then, at
every tick of the clock, it jumps forward by $k$ pads:

- at time $t = 1$, it sits on pad $k$;
- at time $t = 2$, it sits on pad $2k$;
- at time $t = t_0$, it sits on pad $t_0 k$.

A friendly shooter, armed with a water gun, wants to make sure the
frog gets a splash. The catch is this: the shooter must write down
the *entire* plan in advance, *before* the frog chooses $k$. A
plan is a list of shots, where each shot is a pair

<div style={{textAlign:'center', margin:'8px 0'}}>
(lily pad number, time).
</div>

So a plan might begin: "at $t = 1$, shoot at pad $5$; at $t = 2$,
shoot at pad $3$; …".

The question is: *can the shooter devise a single, universal plan
that guarantees the frog gets wet, no matter which number $k$ the
frog chooses?*

## Cracking the puzzle

At first glance this seems impossible. The frog has infinitely
many choices for $k$, yet the plan must be committed to paper in
advance. How can a single fixed list of shots hope to anticipate
every one of those infinitely many trajectories?

Let us restate the situation carefully. The shooter's plan is a
list of shots $S_1, S_2, S_3, \ldots$, where
$S_i = (\mathrm{pad}_i, \mathrm{time}_i)$. If the frog picks $k$,
its trajectory is the set of pairs

$$
P_k = \{(k, 1),\ (2k, 2),\ (3k, 3),\ \ldots\}.
$$

The shooter wins if, for *every* choice of $k$, at least one shot
$S_i$ lies in $P_k$.

Here is the key observation. Although the frog has infinitely many
possible choices, those choices can be arranged in a single list:
they are simply $1, 2, 3, \ldots$, and we have a natural way to go
through them one after another. A set whose elements can be
arranged in such a list is called **listable**, or equivalently
**countable** (or **countably infinite** when the set is infinite).
Listability is what lets the shooter address each choice of $k$ in
turn, using the tick of the clock as the "position" in the list.

This suggests the following strategy:

<div style={{textAlign:'center', fontStyle:'italic', margin:'12px 0'}}>
at time $t$, target the frog that chose $k = t$, that is, shoot at pad $t^2$.
</div>

Spelling this out:

- at $t = 1$, address the frog that chose $k = 1$ by shooting at pad $1^2 = 1$;
- at $t = 2$, address the frog that chose $k = 2$ by shooting at pad $2^2 = 4$;
- at $t = 3$, address the frog that chose $k = 3$ by shooting at pad $3^2 = 9$;
- $\vdots$

Why does this work? Suppose the frog chose some specific $k$; its
position at time $t$ is $tk$. Consider the particular moment
$t = k$:

- *shooter's action at time $t = k$*: shoots at pad $k^2$;
- *frog's position at time $t = k$*: pad $k \cdot k = k^2$.

The two coincide, and the frog gets soaked. Whatever $k$ the frog
might have picked, the shooter's pre-written plan includes a shot
at exactly the right pad at exactly the right instant to meet it.

The trick worked because the frog's infinitely many options could
be lined up in a list, and the clock gave the shooter enough ticks
to address them one by one. This idea — that an infinite set of
choices can sometimes be enumerated, and then dealt with in turn —
is the thread we follow through the rest of this chapter.
